09 Coal and air pollution

Coal-heavy power is a public-health problem.

Like Switzerland, Taiwan is mountainous—but nearly 60% of Swiss power is hydro and nearly 40% nuclear. At end-2018, Taiwan’s installed capacity was about 74.6% thermal, 8.9% hydro, 8.6% nuclear, 8.0% non-hydro renewables.

Within thermal, coal was about 36.6%. Central Taiwan’s air quality has long been a political battlefield—if the air improved, the battlefield would shrink, which may be one reason it never quite does.

Late in the campaign I asked volunteers to open CEMS stack data: flow, opacity, who the real big emitters were. That analysis should be government’s long-term work; too often it becomes a candidate’s pre-election amateur stats.

The conclusion is humble and heavy: under blue–green trench warfare, central Taiwan’s pollution rarely gets a clean day. The wealthy buy purifiers; public-school children still study in the open air.

Energy transition is not abstract ‘green.’ It is whether people can breathe. Every kilowatt-hour’s source ends up in someone’s lungs.
