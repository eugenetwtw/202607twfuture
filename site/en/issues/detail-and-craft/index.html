03 Devil in the details

Craft culture shows up in every joint, not in slogans.

On the HSR project, my direct boss Arnold was a Swiss engineer: courteous, long-haired like a wanderer, no smoking or drinking—and extremely demanding. He told me: ‘Your structural design should have no bending moments at any joint.’

In plain language: the devil is in the details. I was designing a temporary warehouse that would be torn down after the job; he still required that. Through later typhoons and quakes, that warehouse stayed the driest, toughest building on site.

Their firm made prestressed cables and tendons: small volume, durable, high precision, high unit price. Few riders know that on the Taipei–Hsinchu HSR stretch, half the route embeds Swiss-watch-level precision.

Arnold also talked education: every engineering graduate should be able to complete a whole project—he himself had designed and built a small house end to end. Most university teachers in Taiwan never face that ‘finish the whole job’ demand.

Detail is not vain perfectionism; it is a reproducible culture. If Taiwan wants high value-added, detail must become ordinary—not the exception.
