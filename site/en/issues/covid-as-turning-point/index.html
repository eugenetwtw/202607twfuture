08 COVID as a turning point

A rare moment to rethink supply chains and systems.

After returning from Chicago, I wrote a book each year to track progress or regress and push new ideas to the public. In 2019 I ran for the legislature—for someone who chases efficiency and output, campaigns can feel painfully inefficient.

Much of this book began as campaign ideas. When COVID-19 exploded in 2020 and Taiwan managed well, some of those ideas looked more valuable and feasible sooner than expected.

As in 1997 when Taiwan dodged the Asian financial crisis, this time the island’s capacity became more widely known. I see it as one of the most unusual openings in four or five centuries of Taiwan’s history.

Old campaign aesthetics—handshakes, crowds, night-market vote-hunting—were questioned overnight; remote work, masks, and supply-chain reshuffles became daily life. We were forced to redefine ‘effective’ and ‘responsible.’

An opening does not automatically become results. It only offers a window: redesign energy, land, healthcare, and work with reason—or let the window close and return to pollution, long hours, and low-income inertia.
