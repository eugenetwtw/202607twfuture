Issue 26 ≈ 2–3 min read From the book: 7. Blockchain, elections & governance

26 Blockchain voting

Can peer-to-peer lower the cost of democracy?

Swiss citizens hold unusual power: direct democracy, thresholds for constitutional change and vetoing laws that, scaled to Taiwan’s population, are not unimaginable. If citizen power correlates with income, why not seriously design for a bit more power?

Blockchain removes middlemen peer-to-peer. If phones can vote securely, is that more democratic? That is one of this book’s core political-tech questions.

Taiwan is a typhoon- and quake-prone island with a short, turbulent history—and a relatively rare base of human rights. Tech should serve participation, not manipulation.

Phone voting’s opponent is not romantic traditional voting but vote-buying, barriers, information gaps, and system attack risk. Engineering and institutions must solve them together.

On the trail I saw efficiency fight populism. If democracy only runs on handshakes and fatigue campaigns, top talent will not enter—tech can rewrite the cost of participation.
