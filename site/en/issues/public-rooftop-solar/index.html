10 Public rooftop solar

Public roofs plus a public-law body to cut friction.

Taiwan sits at mid–low latitudes with good solar potential; the bottleneck is often land. Putting panels on ponds or farm structures is debatable, but I think the bolder move is rooftops of government offices and schools—and public land.

Civil servants’ reluctance is rational—more duty and risk without more pay. So do not only order them to ‘do more’; use cleaner processes: administrative or public-interest corporations dedicated to planning and installation.

Such entities can separate expertise, procurement, O&M, and risk from ordinary bureaucracy, turning solar into scalable projects instead of every agency reinventing the wheel.

Renewables need speed. Public rooftops are visible, measurable, and relatively low-conflict—do what we can first, then tackle harder land-use and ecological fights.

Sunlight is a public good; roofs are idle capacity. What we lack is institutional design, not latitude.
