02 High-value economy

High GDP comes from high-value services and precision, not bulk materials.

High national GDP is like high output per employee at a firm. Manufacturing must count raw materials, so value-added is hard to lift; services—especially high-end finance, insurance, and healthcare—scale value more readily.

Switzerland turned geographic and political neutrality into long-term conditions for finance; watches show another path: productivity and packing value into tiny objects. Rationality lives not only in philosophy classrooms but in business models and tolerances.

Taiwan’s healthcare and NHI coverage are, in many ways, better than Switzerland’s expensive system. But hardware manufacturing alone will not catch Swiss income levels. Finance, insurance, and high-value services are the next layer that can grow.

Before the pandemic, tourism was also a green, low-carbon high-end service. Swiss mountains, insurance, and risk industries are bound together—a reminder that industry choice is also cultural choice.

If Taiwan wants to ‘become today’s Switzerland,’ that is not slogan-driven transformation. It means steadily steering resources toward exportable intellect, trustworthy institutions, and compact, high-value capability.
