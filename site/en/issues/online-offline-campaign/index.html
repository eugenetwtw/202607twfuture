33 Online/offline campaign labs

A toolbox without factions.

Taiwan counts ballots with startling speed: about 14.3 million valid presidential votes, roughly half a day from polls to a clear result—an island that knows the drill.

Online: Facebook and blogs first; November materials, heavy night-market work only in December. After Google left political ads, precise placement got harder; Line and web ads were imprecise; often only Facebook remained.

Offline: one billboard (NT$6,000 on a neighbor’s balcony), about 30,000 tissue packs, hand placards, volunteers’ cars and my Tesla converted for campaigning—Control Yuan still barred listing the car as expenditure. I considered drones as ad trucks; volume was not enough.

I had used Google Maps for engineering routes nine years earlier; using it to plan visits and read neighborhood structure was data decision, not gut feel.

Campaigns cost money—but you can also run without spending much. What is scarce is people who combine policy, marketing, and fundraising—and such people often have better life options. That is the observation I want left: lower the humiliation cost for first-rate talent to enter.
